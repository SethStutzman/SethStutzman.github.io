“Defense wins championships,” they say. But how true is this statement? Do championship teams actually have top tier defenses? Let’s look at NCAA men’s basketball data from the past five years to find out.

The month of March is a time every year that is highly anticipated for college basketball fans. March Madness is a tournament that reveals the best programs in the country. Teams go head to head until the best one is standing in the NCAA National Championship.

But what contributes the most to the success of these teams that make deep runs in the tournament? Is it an efficient offense or is it a lock down defense? If defense wins championships, the teams that go far should have some of the top defenses in the country.

To look more into this, let’s look at where the Final Four teams from the last five years have ranked in overall efficiency when it comes to scoring and to playing defense.

Over the last five years, every single Final Four team has ranked near the top in overall efficiency. Knowing this, it would appear that offense and defense evenly contribute to a team’s success. But let’s keep looking into the defensive side of the ball to find out more.

Now we will take a look at where championship teams rank in the country. Defensive efficiency measures how many points a team gives up every 100 possessions. Teams with a lower defensive efficiency have more success on the defensive side of the ball. With this in mind, let’s see where the last five national champions have landed in regard to defensive efficiency

Code 
TeamPos <- totalcollegedata |>
  mutate(
    Possessions = field_goals_attempted - offensive_rebounds + total_turnovers + (0.475 * free_throws_attempted)
  )


TeamDefEff <- TeamPos |>
  mutate(
    defensive_efficiency = ((opponent_team_score * 100) / Possessions) /100
  ) |>
  group_by(team_short_display_name, season) |>
  summarize(
    avg_def_eff = mean(defensive_efficiency, na.rm = TRUE),
    games_played = n()
  ) |>
  ungroup() |>
  filter(team_short_display_name %in% c(
    "Illinois", "Indiana", "Iowa", "Maryland", "Michigan", "Michigan State",

Every single national champion from the last five years has ranked inside the top 15 defenses when it comes to defensive efficiency with four of them landing inside the top 10. This is helpful because it suggests that teams who are good enough to win championships consistently hold their opponents to lower scores.

Rebounding is a huge part of basketball. Teams that can pull down more rebounds are more likely to have more success overall. Knowing this, let’s look at how these championship teams rank in rebounding on the defensive side compared to the Big Ten, Pac 12, SEC, ACC, Big 12, and Big East champions of that given year.
